# Sheet Vinyl Flash Coving — Research Report

## TL;DR for the animation fix

The current animation is wrong because flash coving is **not** "sheet vinyl bent at 90°." It is a **layered assembly** — a rigid cap strip at the top, a ~7/8" radius cove stick at the bottom junction, and the sheet wrapping over both — installed in that order, with a heat-welded weld rod at corners. Any showcase that skips the cove stick, skips the cap strip, or shows the vinyl "growing" out of the floor will read as fake to a pro.

---

## 1. Anatomy of a Flash Cove

Flash coving turns a sheet of resilient flooring up the wall in one continuous piece to form an integral, seamless base. Standard rise is **4"–6"**, code-capped at roughly **8"**. Above that it becomes wall covering and must meet wall fire ratings.

### Components (wall-in)

| # | Component | What it is | Typical spec |
|---|-----------|------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Wall substrate** | Must be sound, rigid, smooth, dry | Plaster, drywall/gypsum, plywood, hardboard, cement board, or smooth concrete. Drywall IS acceptable if non-springy. Add a 1" wood ground strip if studs are spaced too wide to fasten the cap strip |
| 2 | **Cove stick / cove former / fillet** | Radiused strip at floor/wall junction — creates the smooth moppable curve | **~7/8" radius North-American standard** (wood, plastic, or wax). Altro metric formers: **CF20R (20 mm)** and **CF38R (38 mm)**. Forbo accepts 20 mm / 35 mm, plus "pencil cove" at 10 mm |
| 3 | **Cap strip / capping bead** | Rigid trim fastened to wall at top of the cove, captures and terminates the sheet edge | Two families: **top-set cove cap** (sheet tucks behind a lipped profile) and **capping bead / Altro Captile C8** (sheet trapped under a lip — used where cove meets wall cladding). Nailed to studs or contact-adhered at chalked height |
| 4 | **Sheet goods** | Turned up the wall in one continuous piece over the cove stick, terminated under the cap strip | 4"–6" standard rise, 8" max per code. Warmed with a heat gun during tucking to prevent cracking |
| 5 | **Flash-cove adhesive** | Wall-grade, non-slumping — NOT field adhesive | Industry reference: **S-581 Flash Cove Adhesive**. Two coats, brush/roller, 4" onto floor + full cove face, 20–30 min between coats |
| 6 | **Heat-welded weld rod** | Color-matched PVC rod melted into grooved seams so the whole floor reads as one piece | Required at every field seam AND at inside/outside corner fills |
| 7 | **Corner fills** | Cut-and-fit pieces or factory pre-fab corners | Inside corners: knife-cut net-fit. Outside corners: **mitered** (heavy-gauge vinyl) or **V-plug / butterfly** (lighter material). **Pre-fab corners** available from FlashCove Prefabricated Bases Inc. and Nora |

### Build order (correct sequence — this is what an animation should follow)

1. Prep wall and floor — smooth, dry, sound; patch imperfections (they telegraph through)
2. **Chalk the cap-strip height line** around the room (4" or 6")
3. **Install the cap strip** to the wall
4. **Install the cove stick** along the floor/wall junction, mitred tight at corners
5. **Template with felt paper** at every inside/outside corner (Template No. 1 for profile, Template No. 2 for corner radius)
6. Cut the sheet using the templates, leaving safety relief cuts at inside corners
7. Apply flash-cove adhesive (two coats)
8. Set the sheet — tuck inside corners first, lap outside second
9. **Warm with heat gun** and tuck firmly into the cove radius, then up under the cap strip — center of each wall first, working out
10. Cut and fit outside corner fills (mitered or V-plug); hand-fit inside corners net
11. Roll the cove with a hand roller, field with a 100 lb roller
12. **Groove and heat-weld every seam and corner fill; skive flush**

## 1b. The three flash cove install methods (one-piece / two-piece / border)

**Verified 2026-04-07** by sheet-vinyl-expert sub-agent against the FlooringTech (CFI) commercial vinyl curriculum and FMH Flooring Chapter 8. The original v1 of this research file omitted this taxonomy — added on the second pass after a user correction.

The CFI commercial vinyl curriculum recognizes **three** flash cove install methods. They produce visually identical finished floors but differ in how the sheet pieces are cut, laid, and seamed. Choice depends on room geometry, roll width, contrasting-colour requirements, and installer preference.

### Method 1: One-piece cove
A single piece of sheet runs continuously from the wall (terminated under the cap strip) → over the cove stick → across the entire floor. **No seam in the cove area.** Field seams (if any) are placed wherever the room is wider than the roll, typically in the middle of the room where they are least visible and least walked on.

- **Use when:** Room is narrow enough that one roll width covers the floor in the cove direction
- **Pro:** No seam near the cove = no risk of weld failure at the most water-exposed area
- **Con:** Wrestling a full-width roll around inside corners is the hardest part of any install; one-piece coving is unforgiving for trainees
- **Common in:** Small bathrooms, cleanrooms, single-loaded corridors, narrow lab benches

### Method 2: Two-piece flash cove
The wall + cove + immediate floor are cut as a separate **cove piece** that extends a short distance (typically ~4"/100mm) onto the floor. The rest of the floor is covered by the **field piece**, which butts up to the cove piece at a heat-welded seam. This is what the YCI marketing site animation shows.

- **Use when:** Room is wider than the roll, OR when the installer wants to isolate the difficult cove work into a manageable narrow strip
- **Pro:** Better control of the hardest part of the install — cove cuts and corner work happen on a small piece in a small workspace, then the easy field is laid in clean straight runs
- **Con:** Adds one heat-weld seam very close to the wall; that seam must be flawless because it sits in the splash zone
- **Common in:** Most commercial healthcare and food-service installs over 2m wide; this is the "default" method for experienced commercial crews

### Method 3: Border flash cove
A variant of two-piece in which the cove piece is **a different colour** from the field, creating a visible coloured border around the room. Same install mechanics as Method 2, but the seam is now an aesthetic feature (visible by design) rather than a hidden join.

- **Use when:** The spec calls for a contrasting border (common in 1950s–80s heritage hospitals, schools, gyms, and premium architectural installs)
- **Pro:** Visual frame around the room; can hide minor wall-out-of-square issues by absorbing them into the border width
- **Con:** Needs extra material, slower layout, two adhesive systems on hand
- **Common in:** Renovation work matching original heritage installs; some modern architectural specs that want a "halo" effect

### Where the seam sits in two-piece / border methods

**~100mm (~4 inches) from the cove base.** Not arbitrary — that's the **published flash-cove adhesive footprint**. Altro, Forbo, Floor Expert, and AHF S-581 data sheets all specify the wall-grade flash-cove adhesive is brushed/rolled "4 inches onto the floor" from the cove. The seam naturally lands at the boundary where the wall-grade adhesive transitions to the field adhesive.

**No manufacturer publishes a minimum-distance-from-wall rule for seams.** Tarkett iQ's "no seams within 0.5 m of floor drains" rule does NOT extend to walls or coves. The seam just has to be properly grooved (~2/3 sheet thickness deep), heat-welded with colour-matched rod, and skived flush. Wall-area seams and field-area seams get treated identically.

### What the marketing animation should show (by method)

| Method | Animation visual | Seam visible? |
|---|---|---|
| One-piece | Single line draws on continuously from floor right edge → up wall to cap | No seam at cove (only mid-floor seams if room is wide) |
| **Two-piece** ✓ | Two pieces draw in from opposite directions, meet at the seam ~100mm from cove base | Yes, hidden join at the heat-weld marker |
| Border | Same as two-piece, but cove piece rendered in a contrast colour | Yes, visible by design |

## 2. Common Animation / Diagram Inaccuracies to AVOID

1. **No cove stick / fillet shown.** Sheet bending into a sharp 90° — or curving in mid-air without a backer — is the #1 tell.
2. **No cap strip at the top.** An unterminated edge feathering into the wall is physically impossible; it would peel.
3. **Wrong radius.** Pencil-thin or ~2"+ radii look cartoonish. Target ~7/8" or ~20 mm visually.
4. **Sheet "growing up" the wall from nothing.** Correct order is substrate → cap strip → cove stick → sheet. The sheet arrives last and covers the assembly.
5. **No weld rod at corners.** The welded seam is a signature visual of real commercial sheet vinyl.
6. **Coving onto any wall without acknowledging prep.** Substrate must be sound and non-springy — the "just bend it against a wall" look misses the story.
7. **Coving shown above ~8".** Above that is wall covering, not base.
8. **Sheet shown at uniform cartoon thickness.** Real sheet vinyl is 2–3 mm — exaggerating thickness destroys the "monolithic" read.
9. **Missing the heat-welded field seam in the floor.** The weld line is part of the sell, not an embarrassment.

## 3. Recommended Visual Showcase Approaches (Ranked)

Goal: communicate "seamless, hygienic, one-piece base" in < 3 seconds on a 390 px mobile card.

### #1 — Annotated cutaway cross-section (RECOMMENDED)
Side-view slice through the floor/wall junction, labeled: wall → cap strip → cove stick (radius called out) → sheet vinyl → floor. Static or gently animated (fade labels in).
- **Pros:** Unambiguous, accurate, reads "spec sheet quality," mobile-friendly, no motion-sickness risk
- **Cons:** Less flashy than a full build animation

### #2 — "With cove vs. without" side-by-side
Left: traditional 90° floor + rubber base with visible seam/gap. Right: flash-coved monolithic floor. Hover/tap to compare.
- **Pros:** Communicates the *benefit* not the process. How Altro/Tarkett actually sell it
- **Cons:** Needs two quality renders; doesn't teach anatomy

### #3 — Exploded isometric
3D axonometric with cap strip, cove stick, sheet, weld rod pulled apart then snapping together.
- **Pros:** Visually rich, showcases craftsmanship
- **Cons:** Heavier asset; harder on 390 px mobile

### #4 — Hover-reveal on a real photo
Flat product photo where hover/tap reveals the hidden cove stick + cap strip.
- **Pros:** Feels concrete
- **Cons:** Needs good reference photography; hover doesn't exist on mobile

### #5 — Full build-order animation (current — lowest priority)
- **Pros:** Tells the whole story
- **Cons:** Easy to get wrong in ways pros notice instantly, slow to load, distracts from the benefit. **Only pursue if co-built with an installer**

**Recommendation for YCI card:** Ship **#1 cutaway diagram** first (fast, accurate, cheap). Layer **#2 with/without comparison** in a secondary slot. Park the current build animation until it can be rebuilt correctly.

## 5. Standards, Codes, Canadian Context

- **Height limit:** Flash coves typically capped at ~8" rise by building codes. Above that → wall covering fire ratings
- **IIC / IBC:** Flash-coved bases are classified similarly to baseboard trim at code-compliant heights
- **USDA / HACCP:** Integrated flash cove + wall cladding systems (Altro Whiterock + Altro floor) are HACCP-approved for food processing and commercial kitchens
- **Infection control:** Referenced in International Health Facility Guidelines Part D (Surfaces & Finishes) as the preferred floor/wall detail for critical care, OR, lab, pharma
- **TTMAC / NFCA / Canadian:** **[UNVERIFIED]** — no Canadian-specific flash cove guide surfaced. NFCA (Canada) and TTMAC (tile) publish standards but no dedicated Canadian flash-cove protocol located. Manufacturer instructions (Altro Canada, Tarkett, Forbo) are the operative specs
- **Ontario / Ottawa:** No province-specific additions found. Ontario Building Code defers to manufacturer installation instructions for resilient flooring systems

- "no maintenance" — false
