# Specialty (More…)

> The "More…" card on the YCI marketing site. Six sub-cards expand on tap in a 2x3 accordion modal. Each sub-card is a specialty flooring category that isn't big enough for its own top-level card but which Young Carpets installs regularly, profitably, and across Ottawa's institutional book.

## What it is
Six specialty flooring categories under one card:
1. Epoxy (resinous flooring)
2. Raised access floor
3. Stair treads + nosings
4. Vinyl + rubber wall base
5. Entrance matting systems
6. ESD / static-dissipative flooring

Plus another dozen sub-specialties (bamboo, cork, sports, PIP playground, lab benches, transition strips, subfloor systems, recoats) that YCI installs on request but which live under other product cards or are too project-specific to merchandise.

The "More…" card is YCI's proof-of-breadth pitch — *"whatever the spec calls for, we install it."*

## Sub-category 1 of 6 — EPOXY (resinous flooring)

### What it is
Cold-applied liquid resin systems that cure into a seamless, chemically resistant monolithic floor. Built up in layers directly on prepared concrete substrate. No seams, no grout lines, no bacterial harbourage. The industry-standard commercial floor wherever hygiene, chemical exposure, or point-load abrasion matters.

Three families YCI quotes:
- **Self-levelling epoxy** — poured neat, trowelled, allowed to self-flow. Smooth matte or gloss finish. Typical 60-125 mil build.
- **Broadcast / quartz systems** — base coat → broadcast coloured quartz aggregate into wet resin → vacuum → seal coat → top coat. Heavy-duty, decorative, slip-resistant. 1/8" to 1/4" build.
- **Urethane cement / mortar** — high-build polyurethane-modified cementitious mortar (Stonhard Stonclad UT, Sika Sikafloor PurCem). The go-to for food processing, breweries, dairies — tolerates thermal shock, steam cleaning, fats and acids.

### Install techniques
- Shot-blast or diamond-grind substrate to CSP 3-4 (ICRI guideline)
- Moisture test per ASTM F2170 (in-situ RH probes) — reject substrates above 75% RH unless system is rated
- Prime with matching epoxy primer
- Apply body coat(s) per manufacturer mil thickness
- Broadcast aggregate to refusal (broadcast systems)
- Vacuum loose aggregate at cure
- Topcoat with clear aliphatic urethane or epoxy novolac for chemical duty
- Integral coved base (4"-6") for wet rooms and hygienic corners
- Recoat cycle: 5-10 years on top coat (does not require full demolition)

### Best used for
- Pharmaceutical and life-sciences labs (Health Canada, NRC, University of Ottawa, uOttawa Heart Institute)
- Federal government mech rooms and chiller plants
- Parking garages and ramp decks (Stonhard Stondeck, Sika Sikafloor-375)
- Food processing and commercial kitchens (PurCem / Stonclad UT)
- Breweries and distilleries (Ottawa craft scene — Beyond the Pale, Dominion City)
- Hospital mech rooms, sterilization suites, morgues (The Ottawa Hospital, CHEO)
- Vet clinics and kennels
- Warehouse and distribution centres (Amazon YOW1 ring, Kanata industrial)
- Data centre battery / UPS rooms
- Schools — shops, science labs, fabrication rooms

## Sub-category 2 of 6 — RAISED ACCESS FLOOR

### What it is
Modular flooring system consisting of removable panels supported on adjustable pedestals (and optional stringers) to create an under-floor plenum for cable management, HVAC supply air, and service distribution. Panels lift out with a suction cup for instant access. Industry-standard in data centres, trading floors, and any modern office that hasn't committed to the ceiling for all its services.

Panel types:
- **Steel cementitious composite** — 24"x24" welded steel shell filled with lightweight cement. The data-centre default. Tate ConCore, Haworth TecCrete.
- **Calcium sulphate (anhydrite)** — dense gypsum-core panel faced with steel. European favourite (Lindner). Heavier, more inert, acoustically deader.
- **Woodcore** — particleboard core faced with galvanized steel and/or aluminum. Office-grade, cheaper, lower load rating.
- **All-steel** — hollow welded shell, no fill. Lightest weight, highest point load. Premium.

Finishes bonded/loose-laid to panel:
- Anti-static HPL laminate (desk-office look)
- Carpet tile (office look, cable management without ESD requirement)
- Vinyl (medical, light clean)
- Porcelain tile (executive / lobby)
- ESD vinyl or rubber (data centre floor tiles with static-dissipative finish — see sub-category 6)

### Install techniques
- Finished floor height (FFH) typically 6"-24"; higher for chilled-water data halls
- Pedestal base epoxied to slab, locknut levelled to laser plane
- Stringers bolted pedestal-to-pedestal (bolted grid) for seismic / heavy-load zones
- Panels drop in loose, held by gravity and edge trim
- Understructure sealed and painted white (data hall airflow)
- Ramps, steps and transition trim at floor-change perimeter
- Cable cut-outs with grommets for each server rack footprint
- Seismic bracing per local code

### Best used for
- **Federal government data centres** — Shared Services Canada consolidation sites. Confirm YCI has the bonding and clearance to bid federal IT-space work.
- **Commercial colocation** — Equinix OT1 (Ottawa), Cologix, eStruxture
- **Trading floors / financial back office** — less common in Ottawa than Toronto, but CMHC, EDC, Farm Credit Canada all run dealing rooms
- **Government command centres** — DND, RCMP, CSE, NRC
- **Modern office interiors** with underfloor air distribution (UFAD) — LEED Gold/Platinum buildings
- **University research computing centres** — uOttawa, Carleton, NRC campus
- **MRI control rooms** (paired with ESD finish)
- **Broadcast / studio master control**

### Suppliers

Tate ConCore steel cementitious is the data-centre default spec in North America. Canadian distribution via Kingspan's reps and authorized installers. ## Sub-category 3 of 6 — STAIR TREADS + NOSINGS

### What it is
Pre-moulded rubber or vinyl stair treads with a cast-in abrasive/contrasting nosing strip, applied over concrete, steel pan, or wood stair substrates. The single most-specified stairwell finish in institutional Ontario — it protects the concrete, defines the nosing visually for accessibility, provides slip resistance, and is individually replaceable as it wears.

Product types YCI installs:
- **Rubber stair tread, square nose** — for closed risers where the stringer is concealed
- **Rubber stair tread with integrated riser** (Johnsonite Angle Fit / Tarkett Angle Fit) — one-piece moulded tread + riser for steel-pan stairs
- **Vinyl stair tread** — lower cost, typically schools and light institutional
- **Photoluminescent nosing strips** — glow-in-the-dark egress marking per NFPA 101 / OBC
- **Aluminum extrusion nosings** — anodized or mill-finish, with rubber/vinyl/epoxy insert; used on terrazzo, stone, tile stairs
- **Abrasive carborundum insert** (Musson specialty) — the gritty black strip; heavy transit / industrial

### Install techniques
- Substrate prep — grind concrete to CSP 2, scrape existing adhesive, patch voids and honeycombing
- Dry-fit every tread and scribe to fit (stringers are rarely square)
- Adhesive — two-part epoxy (high-traffic) or pressure-sensitive tread adhesive (standard)
- Roll tread firmly, especially the nosing return (the peel-up failure point)
- Caulk nosing return bead to substrate (prevents water ingress and grit migration)
- Replace worn treads individually without disturbing adjacent — major lifecycle advantage
- Skirting and stringer rubber wraps per architect

### Best used for
- **Every institutional stairwell in Ottawa**, bar none:
  - Schools — OCDSB, OCSB, CECCE, CEPEO buildings
  - Hospitals — TOH (General, Civic, Riverside), CHEO, Montfort, Queensway-Carleton
  - Federal buildings — PSPC inventory, GAC, DND, CRA, StatsCan
  - Transit — OC Transpo stations, O-Train LRT stations (Confederation Line, Trillium Line)
  - Parking garages — municipal, hospital, university
  - Universities / colleges — uOttawa, Carleton, Algonquin, La Cité
  - Municipal — rec centres, libraries, city hall
  - Office towers — stairwell upgrades during base-building refreshes

### Suppliers

Full product line: Angle Fit rubber stair treads, Angle Fit with integrated riser, Color Splash treads with risers, square-nose treads, stair nosings, photoluminescent egress nosings. Tarkett Commercial runs Canadian distribution and YCI is a known Tarkett/Johnsonite account. This is the #1 spec on 80%+ of Ottawa institutional stairwell work.

## Sub-category 4 of 6 — VINYL + RUBBER WALL BASE

### What it is
Resilient base moulding that wraps the transition between finished floor and wall. The smallest line item on almost every commercial flooring bid, and the most universal — every carpet tile job, every sheet vinyl job, every LVT job gets wall base. YCI runs a continuous back-order of 4" and 6" base in a half-dozen standard colours for top-up and warranty work.

Profiles:
- **Cove base (toed)** — flexible foot wraps onto the floor ~5/8", used over finished concrete, resilient, and hard substrates where a watertight transition is required
- **Straight base (toeless)** — no foot, crisp right angle at the floor; used over carpet tile where the base rests on the carpet pile
- **Millwork base** — thicker, taller profiles that look like painted wood trim but are rubber (Johnsonite Millwork Wall Base System)
- **Sculpted / reveal profiles** — designer profiles for feature walls

Materials:
- **TV — Thermoplastic Vinyl (PVC)** — lowest cost, widest colour range, most common
- **TP — Thermoplastic Rubber (TPR)** — mid-range, better heat/chemical resistance
- **TS — Thermoset Rubber (vulcanised)** — premium, best abrasion/chemical resistance, flattest profile; Johnsonite BaseWorks, Roppe Pinnacle
- **Carpet base** — 2-1/2" carpet-faced for carpet-base-to-carpet-floor continuity

Heights: 2-1/2", 4" (default), 6", and custom tall. Thickness typically 0.080" or 0.125".

### Install techniques
- Roll out coil stock (100' or 120' rolls) or individual 4' pieces
- Acclimate 24-48 hours to room temp
- Apply base cement with notched trowel (cove adhesive specifically formulated, not multi-purpose)
- Press foot firmly into floor corner — the single most common install defect is a lifting toe
- Butt joints tight — outside corners mitred or preformed, inside corners scribed tight
- Roll the top edge firmly — the second most common defect is a base that peels at the top
- Wipe adhesive squeeze-out immediately

### Best used for
- Literally every commercial flooring job YCI does that isn't ceramic, terrazzo, or polished concrete
- Schools, hospitals, offices, retail, government, light industrial
- Paired with: carpet tile, sheet vinyl, LVT, linoleum, rubber flooring, epoxy (where an integral cove isn't spec'd)
- NOT used with: integral-cove epoxy systems, terrazzo with integral cove, ceramic base tile

### Suppliers

Traditional Vinyl Base thermoplastic (Type TV). Millwork Wall Base finishing system for premium trim looks. Over 100 colours. Confirmed YCI Tarkett account.
- **Roppe** (verified) — Pinnacle thermoset rubber base is the direct Johnsonite BaseWorks competitor. Often spec'd by alternate on price-sensitive jobs. Confirm YCI Roppe purchasing channel.
- **Tarkett** (verified) — same corporate parent as Johnsonite; Tarkett-branded wall base (without the Johnsonite sub-brand) shows up on some specs. Same distribution channel.

### Standards
- **ASTM F1861** — Standard Specification for Resilient Wall Base. The product standard. Defines:
  - Type TV (Thermoplastic Vinyl / PVC)
  - Type TP (Thermoplastic Rubber)

## Sub-category 5 of 6 — ENTRANCE MATTING SYSTEMS

### What it is
Engineered mat systems at building entrances that scrape, trap, and absorb dirt, grit, snow, and moisture before it reaches the finish floor. On the inside face of the building envelope, entrance matting is a life-safety, IAQ, and maintenance-cost system — not decor. Every slip-and-fall claim in an Ottawa commercial lobby in February starts with inadequate entrance matting.

The ISSA (International Sanitary Supply Association) rule of thumb: 12-15 feet of effective matting run captures 80% of incoming soil. Ottawa winters push that higher — 15-25 feet recommended on high-volume lobbies.

Three zones of an entrance matting system:
1. **Exterior scraper** — outside the door, typically coir, aluminum grid, or Pedigrid. Scrapes aggressive soil before the door.
2. **Interior primary** — recessed grid or heavy-rib mat inside the vestibule. Scrapes and drops debris below grade.
3. **Interior secondary** — tackified carpet-face mat (Forbo Coral) absorbs residual moisture and fine dust. The "finishing" mat.

Mount types:
- **Recessed** — mat sits in a stainless steel or aluminum frame set into a depression in the slab, flush with the finish floor. Premium, permanent. Requires slab depression at the concrete pour or core-out during renovation.
- **Surface-mounted** — mat with bevelled edges sits directly on the finish floor. Retrofit standard.
- **Roll goods** — Forbo Coral in broadloom or tile, installed like carpet, no frame.

### Install techniques
- Coordinate slab depression early with GC and concrete sub (recessed systems) — 3/8" to 3/4" typical depression depth
- Frame set in thinset or epoxy grout, levelled to finish floor
- Mat sections assembled with hinge bars or clip connectors
- Drainage wells under Pedigrid systems plumbed to floor drain or sloped to weep
- Roll-goods tackified with pressure-sensitive adhesive over concrete or set into recess
- Seasonal rotation for tackified face mats (clean, replace, rotate)

### Best used for
- **Federal government buildings** — lobbies of PSPC-managed buildings, departmental HQs. Government lobbies are high-scrutiny: IAQ compliance, accessibility, slip-rating all mandatory.
- **Shopping centres and malls** — Rideau Centre, Bayshore, St. Laurent, Tanger Kanata, Place d'Orléans
- **Transit station entries** — OC Transpo stations, O-Train LRT entrances (Rideau, Parliament, Tunney's Pasture)
- **Hospital and clinic lobbies**
- **University / college main entrances**
- **Office tower lobbies** (especially downtown Ottawa — Sparks St, Bank St, ByWard)
- **Grocery stores and big-box** (Loblaws, Metro, Costco, Canadian Tire)
- **Museums, galleries, performing arts** (NAC, NGC, Canadian Museum of History)
- **Hotels** (Fairmont Château Laurier, Westin, Marriott)

### Suppliers

Pedimat M1 (surface or recessed, rollable aluminum hinge rails), Pedigrid G1 (aluminum scraper grid with 44 mm deep drainable well), Peditred PR (combined vinyl/carpet insert), PediTile (modular). ## Sub-category 6 of 6 — ESD / STATIC-DISSIPATIVE FLOORING

### What it is
Floor coverings engineered to control electrostatic discharge by providing a controlled path-to-ground for static charges generated by foot traffic, rolling chairs, or equipment movement. Specified where uncontrolled ESD events can destroy electronics, ignite flammable atmospheres, or corrupt sensitive measurements. The smallest and most technically gated of the six sub-categories — the specifier is usually an ESD engineer or a Facility Qualified Engineer (FQE), not an architect.

Two resistance classes (per ANSI/ESD S20.20 and ASTM F150):
- **Conductive (C)** — < 1 × 10⁶ Ω point-to-ground (very fast discharge; used where even mV matters — munitions, explosives)
- **Static Dissipative (SD)** — 1 × 10⁶ to 1 × 10⁹ Ω point-to-ground (controlled discharge rate; the common commercial spec — server rooms, electronics manufacturing, MRI control)

Material formats:
- **Homogeneous ESD vinyl tile** — solid-pigment PVC with carbon veins running through the thickness (Forbo Colorex SD/EC, Staticworx GroundSafe vinyl). The data-centre and electronics-lab default.
- **ESD rubber tile** — Staticworx Eclipse/Aspire; more durable, slip-rated
- **ESD epoxy** — 3-coat system with carbon-loaded topcoat, integral copper grounding grid at perimeter (Stonhard Stontec ESD, Sika SikaFloor ESD, Dur-A-Flex Hybri-Flex ESD)
- **ESD carpet tile** — Shaw ESD, Tandus ESD — office-grade static control, not "true" ESD per S20.20
- **ESD raised access floor panel finish** — ESD vinyl laminate bonded to raised-floor panel face (combines sub-categories 2 and 6)
- **Copper grounding strap + perimeter ground drain** — installed as part of the floor; strap connects to building ground at grounding cell

### Install techniques
- Substrate prep identical to resilient (CSP 2, ASTM F710, moisture per F2170)
- Install perimeter copper grounding strap under the field, every ~1,000 sqft, bonded to electrical ground
- Conductive primer and conductive adhesive (the adhesive is the ground path — standard adhesive will isolate the floor)
- ESD tile or sheet laid with tight seams (welded on sheet goods)
- Post-install resistance testing with calibrated megohmmeter (AEMC 6536 or equivalent), 5 lb NFPA probes at 3 ft separation
- Document point-to-ground and point-to-point values at minimum every 500 sqft
- Issue test report for facility commissioning / ANSI/ESD S20.20 compliance file
- Recurring periodic verification per IEC 61340-5-1 site audit schedule

### Best used for
- **Data centre tech floors** — server halls, network operations, especially in raised-access configuration

### Standards
- **ANSI/ESD S20.20** — **THE** specification. *Protection of Electrical and Electronic Parts, Assemblies and Equipment (Excluding Electrically Initiated Explosive Devices)*. Published by ESDA (EOS/ESD Association). The facility-level ESD program standard; the floor is one element of the overall program. Current revision: ANSI/ESD S20.20-2021. YCI installers should be familiar with the compliance workflow and able to issue post-install test reports that slot into the facility's S20.20 audit file.
- **ASTM F150** — *Standard Test Method for Electrical Resistance of Conductive and Static Dissipative Resilient Flooring*. The tile/sheet resistance test: 5 lb NFPA probes at 3 ft separation, known voltage applied, resistance read. Distinguishes conductive from static-dissipative. Current: ASTM F150-06(2018).
- **IEC 61340-4-1** — *Electrostatics, Part 4-1: Standard test methods for specific applications — Electrical resistance of floor coverings and installed floors*. Measures resistance to ground, point-to-point, and vertical resistance in the 10⁴-10¹³ Ω range. The European/international equivalent of F150, frequently referenced in cleanroom and multinational specs.
- **IEC 61340-5-1** — facility-level ESD control program (European counterpart to S20.20)
- **ESD TR53** — Technical Report on compliance verification (the periodic-audit procedures document)
- **Motorola R56** — Standards and Guidelines for Communication Sites (referenced by telecom)
- **ATIS 0600321** — telecom central office environmental
- **FAA 019f** — FAA air traffic control facility power/grounding
- **MIL-PRF-32752** — US military ESD floor specification
- **NFPA 99** — Healthcare Facilities Code (references static control in anesthetizing locations)

## Maintenance
- **Epoxy** — sweep + damp mop with neutral pH cleaner. Avoid solvent cleaners. Recoat top layer every 5-10 years. Urethane mortar tolerates aggressive steam cleaning.
- **Raised access** — vacuum individual panels, lift and clean cavity annually, replace individual panels as worn/damaged. Verify pedestal torque every 5 years.
- **Stair treads** — sweep, damp mop, neutral cleaner. Replace individual treads as the nosing wears. Reseat adhesive on lifting edges.
- **Wall base** — wipe with damp cloth and neutral cleaner. Reseat lifting toes with base cement. Replace individual sections.
- **Entrance matting** — daily vacuum on tackified carpet-face. Lift Pedigrid panels weekly and clean the well. Rotate/replace tackified mats seasonally.
- **ESD** — neutral pH cleaner ONLY — standard floor finishes and waxes *destroy ESD conductivity* and void S20.20 compliance. Use ESD-approved cleaners. Re-test resistance annually.
