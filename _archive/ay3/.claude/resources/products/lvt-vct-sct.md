# LVT / VCT / SCT

> Three vinyl flooring families under one product card. The marketing site uses a tabbed modal interaction to differentiate them — each tab below maps to a tab in the UI and should carry equal visual weight.

## Overview

Vinyl is the dominant resilient flooring family in commercial Ontario work. Within vinyl there are three distinct sub-products that look similar on a spec sheet but behave completely differently on a jobsite, in maintenance, and in a capital budget:

- **LVT** — the modern, design-led, wear-layered plank or tile. Dominant in new commercial fit-outs since the early 2020s.
- **VCT** — the institutional legacy tile. Cheap, specifiable, and requires a maintenance crew. Still specified constantly in Ontario schools, hospitals, and municipal buildings in 2026.
- **SCT / homogeneous sheet** — the engineering vinyl. Colour runs through the full thickness, seams are heat-welded, walls are flash-coved. The default for Canadian healthcare, labs, and clean rooms.

YCI installs all three. The product tab a customer lands on depends entirely on their environment and who will maintain the floor.

## Tab 1 — LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile / Plank)

### What it is

Multi-layer flexible vinyl with a high-resolution print film topped by a clear urethane-reinforced PVC wear layer. The wear layer is the only part of the plank that sees a shoe — it is the finish, the scratch shield, and the warranty basis in a single film. Visually LVT mimics wood, stone, concrete, terrazzo, and abstract textures at a resolution indistinguishable from the source material at normal walking distance.

LVT in 2026 is the most-specified resilient flooring in Ontario commercial work and has been since roughly 2020. Shaw Industries alone accounts for roughly a third of U.S. LVT consumption.

### Sub-formats (the important distinction)

| Format | Core | Install method | Typical thickness | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Glue-down dryback** | Flexible PVC | Full-spread or pressure-sensitive adhesive | 2.0 – 3.0 mm | High-traffic commercial, rolling loads |
| **Click-lock floating** | Flexible PVC or rigid core | Mechanical edge profile, no adhesive | 4.0 – 6.0 mm | Retrofit, tight schedules, tenant improvements |
| **SPC (Stone Plastic Composite)** | Limestone/PVC rigid core | Click-lock, floating | 4.0 – 6.0 mm | High-traffic commercial, temp-swing spaces |
| **WPC (Wood Plastic Composite)** | Wood flour/PVC foamed core | Click-lock, floating | 5.5 – 8.0 mm | Softer underfoot — hospitality, light retail |
| **Loose-lay** | Heavy rigid backing | Friction + perimeter glue, no field adhesive | 5.0 mm | Fast replacement, raised access floors |

**SPC vs. WPC vs. traditional flexible LVT — the 2026 terminology:**
SPC and WPC are not separate products from LVT — they are **core technologies** that can sit underneath an LVT wear layer and print film. SPC uses a dense limestone-and-PVC composite core, making the plank harder, more dent-resistant, dimensionally stable across temperature swings, and generally cheaper to produce. WPC uses a foamed wood-flour-and-PVC core, producing a plank that is warmer and softer underfoot but less dense. For commercial spec work, SPC is now the dominant rigid-core choice; WPC survives mostly in hospitality and residential. Traditional flexible glue-down dryback LVT remains the choice for the heaviest commercial and healthcare corridor work — rigid cores are wonderful until a hospital bed wheel rolls across a click seam.

### Wear layer — the only number that matters for commercial

Wear layer is measured in mils (1 mil = 0.001 inch = 0.0254 mm). It is the single most important number on an LVT spec sheet for commercial work. The ASTM baseline for a commercial-classified LVT is **20 mil**.

| Wear layer | Use class | Typical application |
|---|---|---|
| 6 – 12 mil | Residential | Not specifiable for commercial — walk away |
| 20 mil | Light commercial | Small offices, boutique retail, low-traffic corridors |
| 22 – 28 mil | Standard commercial | Retail, hospitality, multi-tenant office, education classrooms |
| 30 mil | Heavy commercial | Hospitals, airports, big-box retail, high-traffic corridors |
| 40 mil (1.0 mm) | Ultra-heavy commercial | Specialty transit, convention centre main floors |

YCI's rule of thumb for Ontario commercial spec work: **never specify below 20 mil, default to 22 mil for general commercial, step up to 30 mil for healthcare and education.** Karndean's commercial line runs to 30 mil with a 20-year commercial warranty, which is the benchmark.

### Install patterns

| Pattern | Offset | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Stagger plank (1/3 offset)** | Each row offset 1/3 of plank length | The commercial default. Least waste, most stable, hides end-joint stress |
| **Brick (1/2 offset)** | Each row offset 1/2 plank length | Visually rhythmic but exaggerates substrate waves at end joints |
| **Random stagger** | 6", 12", 18" variable offsets | Most natural wood look — requires planning, produces more waste |
| **Herringbone** | Rectangular planks at 90° forming zigzag | Hospitality, boutique retail, feature corridors. Requires left/right plank SKUs on some brands |
| **Chevron** | Planks mitred to meet in a straight line at an angle | Higher waste, higher labour, highest design impact |
| **Grid tile** | Square tile, straight or quarter-turn | Stone and concrete visuals |

Herringbone and chevron plank layouts have become strongly trending commercial patterns through the mid-2020s, particularly in hospitality and boutique retail. Both require a tighter substrate flatness tolerance and typically a 20 mil or heavier wear layer to handle edge stress at the elevated number of joints.

### Install techniques

| Technique | Substrate | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glue-down dryback (full-spread) | Concrete, plywood, APA underlayment | High-traffic, heavy rolling load, healthcare | Most stable, highest warranty tier. Trowel-applied acrylic adhesive per supplier spec |
| Pressure-sensitive glue-down | Concrete, plywood | Fast turnover retail, phased installs | Adhesive flashes off, tile is set into tacky film. Faster open time |
| Click-lock floating | Any flat substrate | Retrofit over old VCT, tenant improvements | Allows minor substrate movement. No adhesive cost. Perimeter expansion gap required |
| Loose-lay | Smooth flat concrete, raised access floors | Quick tile replacement, data centres | Heavy backed planks, perimeter tackifier or glue. Individual plank replacement is trivial |

### Acoustic-backed variants

Multi-level commercial builds — multi-tenant office over retail, hospitality over lobbies, multi-storey healthcare — frequently require an IIC/STC impact sound rating that bare LVT cannot deliver alone. Three options:

1. **Factory acoustic backing** — cork, IXPE foam, or rubber laminated to the plank at the mill. Cleanest install, highest cost per square foot.
2. **Site-applied acoustic underlayment** — cork roll, rubber roll, or rigid acoustic mat between substrate and LVT. More flexible spec, requires compatible adhesive on top.
3. **Rigid-core SPC with pre-attached IXPE pad** — most common factory acoustic choice for floating installs.

Spec note: acoustic-backed LVT in a glue-down system often requires a moisture-mitigation primer the standard system does not — always check the supplier's backing-specific install guide.

### LVT standards
- **ASTM F1303** — Standard Specification for Sheet Vinyl Floor Covering With Backing (applies to backed sheet and some LVT constructions). Types I/II, Grades 1/2/3, Backing Classes A/B/C.
- **ASTM F1700** — Standard Specification for Solid Vinyl Floor Tile (applies to LVT tile/plank). Class I monolithic, Class II surface-decorated, Class III printed film; Type A smooth, Type B embossed.
- **ASTM F1869 / F2170** — substrate moisture (calcium chloride / RH probe).
- **FloorScore (SCS + RFCI)** — VOC emissions certification, required for LEED v4.1, WELL, BREEAM, CHPS, Green Globes credit.
- **EN 685 Class 33 / 34** — European commercial use area classification; 33 = heavy commercial, 34 = very heavy commercial.

## Tab 2 — VCT (Vinyl Composition Tile)

### What it is

A rigid 12×12×1/8" tile that is mostly limestone filler bound together with a small percentage of vinyl resin, plasticizer, and pigment. VCT is not a finished floor as delivered — the factory surface is matte, porous, and requires a wax finish on top to become a usable, cleanable floor. That wax is the finish, the scuff layer, and the shine. Strip it off and the bare tile is what you have to work with again.

VCT is cheap, tough against impact (it's mostly rock), and endlessly repairable. It is also the lowest-appearance vinyl product on the market until it has been stripped, waxed, and burnished — at which point it becomes the glass-smooth floor everyone recognizes from a 1970s school hallway, a hospital corridor, or a municipal back-of-house.

### Why VCT is still specified in 2026 despite LVT dominance

Five reasons, in order:

1. **Capital cost.** VCT is the cheapest vinyl flooring product on the market, roughly $2–5/sqft installed vs $4–15 for LVT. On a 50,000 sqft school corridor spec, that delta is the difference between winning and losing a public tender.
2. **Institutional maintenance contracts.** School boards, hospitals, and municipal facilities already have union maintenance crews, existing strip/wax equipment, and established product supply contracts. Switching to LVT means retraining, new equipment, and writing off existing supplies.
3. **Repair granularity.** A single damaged tile is a 10-minute replacement with a putty knife, a heat gun, and a fresh tile from the attic. LVT patch repairs are far harder to make invisible.
4. **Impact and rolling load durability.** VCT's limestone body shrugs off dropped tools and heavy rolling loads that would dent a flexible LVT wear layer.
5. **Public tender language.** Many Ontario public works specifications are still written around ASTM F1066 (VCT) because they were written that way in 1995 and never updated. Inertia is a powerful spec force.

### Install — full-spread acrylic adhesive, no alternatives

VCT is installed one way: full-spread acrylic adhesive over a prepared substrate. There is no floating VCT, no click VCT, no loose-lay VCT. Any project drawing that suggests otherwise is wrong.

**Process:**
1. Substrate prep — patch, level to ≤ 3/16" in 10', prime per adhesive manufacturer.
2. Moisture test — calcium chloride (ASTM F1869) or in-situ RH probe (ASTM F2170). Fail = mitigate before proceeding.
3. Acclimate tile 48 hours at ≥ 18 °C / 65 °F.
4. Spread adhesive with the supplier-specified notched trowel. Armstrong-approved adhesives for Standard Excelon are **S-515 Tile Strong**, **S-319 Roll Strong**, **S-1000**, or **PS-820** pressure-sensitive; **PU-100** 2-part polyurethane for areas with frequent surface moisture; **SC-100** solvent contact for accessories and trims.
5. Let flash per adhesive spec.
6. Set tile into adhesive, straight grid or quarter-turn.
7. Roll with 100 lb roller both directions.
8. Protect from traffic until adhesive cure.
9. Strip factory wax and apply finish: 4–5 coats of floor finish, allow 24 hr cure before burnishing.

### Patterns

- **Straight grid** — default. Tile in parallel rows, no quarter turn.
- **Quarter-turn** — every tile rotated 90° from its neighbour. Originally specified to balance directional streaks in the factory pigment; still occasionally called out for historical reasons.
- **Feature stripe / border** — contrasting colour stripe 4, 6, or 12 tiles in from the wall; standard in schools, healthcare, and transit for wayfinding. Full tile inlays for logos or wayfinding graphics are straightforward given the 12×12 grid.

### VCT maintenance protocol — the reason to pick or reject VCT

This is the single most important conversation with a VCT customer. If the customer cannot commit to this maintenance regimen, specify LVT instead.

**Daily:**
- Dust mop with treated mop or microfibre, end of shift.
- Spot-mop spills immediately.
- Walk-off mats at every exterior entry — VCT finish is destroyed by grit.

**Weekly:**
- Damp mop with neutral pH cleaner (NOT ammonia, NOT high-alkaline — these strip finish).
- Auto-scrub with red pad in high-traffic areas.

**Monthly / every few weeks:**
- **Burnish** with a high-speed burnisher (1,500–2,000 RPM) and a burnishing pad to restore gloss and thermally re-bond micro-scratches in the finish. Burnishing is the interim maintenance step that extends the strip/wax cycle.

**Semi-annually (typical school / high-traffic):**
- **Strip and refinish.** Strip old finish with alkaline stripper, rinse/neutralize, apply 4–5 coats of floor finish, allow full cure (24 hours before burnishing), return to service.

**Frequency benchmarks:**
- Schools, municipal lobbies, transit: every 6 months.
- Healthcare corridors: every 4–6 months depending on traffic.
- Low-traffic back-of-house: annually.

### VCT standards
- **ASTM F1066** — Standard Specification for Vinyl Composition Floor Tile. Covers smooth or embossed surface VCT, deflection limit, dimensional stability, chemical resistance, heat resistance.
- **ASTM F1869 / F2170** — substrate moisture.
- **FloorScore** — VOC emissions certification.

## Tab 3 — SCT / Homogeneous Sheet Vinyl

### What it is

**Homogeneous** means the colour, pattern, and wear material run through the entire thickness of the sheet or tile. There is no print film, no separate wear layer, no backing. Sand it, scratch it, chip it — the material underneath looks identical to the surface. Manufacturing is done by calendering a pre-pigmented PVC compound and embossing the finished sheet.

This is the engineering vinyl. It is specified where a print-film LVT cannot be trusted: operating theatres, ICUs, pharmacy clean rooms, isolation rooms, labs, decon zones, commercial kitchens, and food-prep spaces. It is also specified in heavy-wear education spec work where the facility wants 25-year appearance retention without a refinish cycle.

"SCT" is used in North America for the **tile** format per ASTM F1700, but in the Canadian healthcare market the overwhelmingly dominant format is **homogeneous sheet vinyl** — 2 m wide rolls, heat-welded seams, flash-coved up the wall. When a YCI customer says "SCT" in a healthcare context, 9 times out of 10 they mean homogeneous sheet.

### The three differentiators

Every healthcare, lab, and clean-room homogeneous sheet install includes some or all of:

1. **Heat-welded seams** — sheets are butted tight, the seam is routed with a grooving tool, and a matching PVC welding rod is fused into the groove with a hot-air welder at ~400 °C. Cooled, the rod is skived flush with a spatula knife. Result: a continuous, impermeable, chemically bonded seal across the entire floor area. No water, no bacteria, no blood, no solvent penetrates the seam.
2. **Flash-coved walls** — the sheet is rolled up the wall 4–6" over a cove former strip, terminated with a capping profile. No horizontal floor/wall joint exists. No harbourage for dirt or biologicals. Cleaners can pressure-wash the wall/floor junction without compromising the seal.
3. **Full-spread adhesive only** — no floating sheet, no loose-lay. The sheet is rolled into a spread adhesive with a 100 lb roller, flashed, and rolled again.

### Sub-formats

| Format | Typical use |
|---|---|
| **Homogeneous sheet (2 m roll)** | Healthcare, labs, clean rooms, pharmacy — the dominant format |
| **Homogeneous tile** (SCT per ASTM F1700) | Lower-spec installs, small rooms, patch repair |
| **Static-dissipative (SD/EC) sheet** | Electronics assembly, server rooms, MRI, imaging |
| **Conductive sheet** | Operating theatres, anaesthesia rooms |
| **Safety / slip-resistant sheet** (Altro, Polyflor Polysafe) | Commercial kitchens, wet rooms, food prep, showers |
| **Acoustic-backed homogeneous** (e.g., Tarkett iQ Optima Acoustic) | Healthcare multi-level builds |

### Install technique — detailed

1. **Substrate prep** — this is where homogeneous sheet installs succeed or fail. Flatness tolerance is **≤ 1/8" in 10'** for heat-welded seam work — tighter than any other resilient product. Any ridge, hump, or dip is visible through the sheet and will telegraph through the welded seam. Patch, skim-coat, and grind as required.
2. **Moisture test** — mandatory. ASTM F1869 calcium chloride or ASTM F2170 in-situ RH probe. Failure requires a moisture-mitigation primer or epoxy barrier before adhesive.
3. **Acclimation** — unroll sheet at least 24 hours at ≥ 18 °C to relax the roll memory.
4. **Cove former installation** — continuous wood, aluminum, or PVC cove strip adhered to the floor/wall junction at the perimeter wherever flash-coving is specified.
5. **Adhesive spread** — supplier-specified acrylic or epoxy (per chemical-resistance requirement), notched-trowel applied, flashed.
6. **Sheet set** — sheets rolled into adhesive, butted tight at seams, rolled with 100 lb roller in both directions.
7. **Sheet trim at coves** — sheet rolled up the cove former and scribed against the wall, terminated into a cap bead.
8. **Seam groove** — wait the adhesive-specified cure window (typically 24 hours), then route each seam with an electric or manual grooving tool to ~2/3 the sheet thickness.
9. **Heat weld** — matching PVC welding rod fused into the groove at ~400 °C with a hand-held hot-air welder. Bead protrudes above the sheet surface.
10. **Skive** — first pass with a spatula knife and sled trims the bead to just above the sheet surface. Second pass after full cool skives flush.
11. **Inspection** — every seam walked, inspected for voids, skips, or burn-through. Repair sections are cut out and re-welded.

## Cross-cutting — applies to all three sub-products

### Substrate prep (baseline)

| Sub-product | Flatness tolerance | Moisture test | Prep |
|---|---|---|---|
| LVT (glue-down) | ≤ 3/16" in 10' | ASTM F1869 / F2170 | Patch, prime, embossing leveller over old VCT |
| LVT (floating) | ≤ 3/16" in 10' | ASTM F1869 / F2170 | Same, plus vapour barrier if spec requires |
| VCT | ≤ 3/16" in 10' | ASTM F1869 / F2170 | Patch, prime, tile-adhesive-compatible primer |
| Homogeneous sheet | **≤ 1/8" in 10'** | ASTM F1869 / F2170 (strict) | Skim-coat, feather, grind. The substrate is visible through the sheet |

**Moisture failure** is the #1 resilient-flooring call-back in Ontario. Any concrete slab that fails ASTM F1869 (> 3 lb / 1000 sqft / 24 hr) or ASTM F2170 (> 75% RH) requires a moisture-mitigation primer (epoxy, single-component urethane, or two-part moisture barrier) before any adhesive is applied. Skipping this step results in adhesive emulsification, bond failure, and complete floor replacement.

### Best NOT used for

- **LVT:** outdoor, wet submerged, continuous chemical exposure, rolling-load surgical spaces (specify homogeneous). Flexible LVT under 20 mil is not a commercial product regardless of the data sheet.
- **VCT:** any facility without a committed strip/wax/burnish maintenance crew. Full stop. A VCT floor without maintenance is a failed floor within 18 months.
- **Homogeneous sheet:** design-led showrooms, open-plan office feature zones, hospitality where a wood or stone visual is required. Homogeneous sheet is an engineering product, not a design product — the visual palette is intentionally limited.
