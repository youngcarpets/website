# Resinous Flooring (Epoxy / Urethane / PurCem / MMA)

> Four chemistries under one product card. The marketing site presents resinous as a single specialty system with a chemistry-picker, because the buyer rarely walks in asking for "novolac epoxy" — they walk in saying "we need a brewery floor" or "we need a parking-garage deck" and the chemistry follows the use case.

## What it is

Resinous flooring is a **field-applied liquid system** — two- or three-component reactive resin poured, troweled, broadcast, or rolled onto a prepared concrete slab where it chemically cross-links into a continuous, seamless, monolithic wear surface bonded directly to the substrate. Unlike sheet vinyl or rubber it has no factory-made wear layer, no seams, and no joints except where the slab itself moves. Unlike polished concrete it is a coating *over* the slab, not the slab itself — and unlike paint it is a true structural floor build measured in millimetres, not mils.

A project specs resinous when one or more of these is true:
- **Chemical attack** — the floor will see acids, caustics, solvents, hydrocarbons, blood/tissue, sugars, lactic acid (dairy), or food-grade sanitizers
- **Thermal shock** — steam cleaning, hot wash-down, or freezer-to-loading-dock cycling
- **Heavy mechanical impact** — forklifts, pallet jacks, dropped tools, point loads from rolling equipment
- **Hygiene** — seamless, cleanable, with sanitary cove base integral to the floor (food, pharma, healthcare back-of-house)
- **Static control** — ESD-conductive or dissipative systems for electronics, munitions, server rooms
- **Speed of return-to-service** — MMA can be back in production in hours
- **Decorative** — metallic epoxy, broadcast quartz, custom logos, line striping for safety and wayfinding

YCI's relevant context: Canadian commercial flooring contractor (Ottawa). The four chemistries below are all biddable in the Canadian market through national distribution networks; the question is usually whether YCI self-performs or subs to a regional applicator (see Open research questions).

## Types / sub-products

### Epoxy
The cheapest and most common resinous chemistry. Two-part (Part A resin + Part B hardener) thermoset that cures to a hard, glossy, chemically resistant film. Long pot life makes it forgiving to apply, which is why it dominates entry-level industrial floors. Weaknesses: **ambers under UV** (turns yellow at exterior windows and sun-exposed bays), **softens above ~60 °C**, **cracks under thermal shock** (steam wash-down will delaminate it), and is brittle to direct impact compared to PU. Ideal as a primer, body coat, or self-leveller in interior, climate-controlled spaces.

### Polyurethane (PU)
Tougher, more flexible, and more abrasion-resistant than epoxy. Two roles: as a **top-coat over an epoxy body** (most common — 4–8 mil aliphatic urethane gives the system UV stability, scuff resistance, and chemical resistance) or as a **full PU system** where the entire build is urethane (rare, expensive, used where the spec demands maximum abrasion + flexibility). Aliphatic PU is non-yellowing; aromatic PU is cheaper but ambers like epoxy. Required wherever the floor sees rolling traffic + sunlight + chemicals all at once.

### PurCem / cementitious urethane
**The gold standard for breweries, commercial kitchens, dairies, food plants, and any wash-down environment.** A three-component system (PU resin + hardener + cementitious filler) that cures to a dense, matte, slip-resistant matrix that looks more like terrazzo than coating. The killer feature: **handles thermal shock from steam cleaning, hot oil spills, and cold-water blast without delaminating**, because the cementitious filler gives it a thermal expansion coefficient close to the concrete underneath. Also tolerates the aggressive caustics, lactic acid, and sanitizers used in food and beverage CIP cycles. Typical thicknesses 1/4" (6 mm) for general food-plant work, 3/8" (9 mm) for breweries and meat processing where cart wheels and dropped equipment take a beating. Branded as **Sika Sikafloor PurCem**, **Stonhard Stonclad UR**, **Florock FlorClad PurCem**, **Dur-A-Flex Poly-Crete** (now under Sherwin-Williams High Performance Flooring).

### MMA (methyl methacrylate)
**The fast-cure chemistry.** Acrylic-based, two-part (resin + BPO catalyst), cures by free-radical polymerization in **1–2 hours to foot traffic** regardless of substrate temperature down to **−25 °C**. Because it cures in cold and cures fast, it owns the niches where epoxy and PU can't go: **walk-in freezers**, **outdoor parking garage decks installed in winter**, **24/7 facilities that can't take a 48-hour shutdown** (transit stations, hospital corridors, retail rollout reno schedules). The catch: **strong odour during install** — uncured MMA monomer smells like a nail salon at 100× volume and the smell carries for hundreds of feet. Full negative-pressure containment + dedicated exhaust fans + tenant notification required, and on occupied buildings the install usually happens overnight. Worker respirator program is non-negotiable.

## Install techniques

| Technique | Substrate | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Roller-applied seal coat** | Sound concrete, CSP 2–3 | Light-duty warehouse, mech rooms | Cheapest; no slip aggregate unless added |
| **Squeegee + back-roll self-levelling** | Shot-blasted concrete, CSP 3 | Decorative metallic, smooth labs, light manufacturing | Operator skill drives finish quality |
| **Broadcast-to-rejection** | Shot-blasted, CSP 3–4 | Slip-rated decorative, wash-down areas, ramps | Aggregate broadcast into wet body, vacuumed, top-coated |
| **Trowelled mortar** | Shot-blasted or scarified, CSP 4–5 | Heaviest-duty industrial, food, brewery, meat plant | Power-trowel or hand-trowel; thickest, most expensive |
| **Spray-applied PU top** | Cured epoxy or PurCem body | UV top-coat, large floors | HVLP spray; faster than rolling on big areas |
| **Hand-laid sanitary cove base** | Wall-to-floor transition | Food, pharma, healthcare | 4–6" radius, integral to floor system, no seam at base |
| **MMA cold-pour** | Shot-blasted, CSP 4 | Freezers, winter exterior decks, 24/7 facilities | Negative-air containment + respirator program mandatory |

## Best used for

- **Commercial kitchens** — restaurants, hotel kitchens, institutional kitchens, catering halls (PurCem, integral cove)
- **Breweries / distilleries / wineries** — fermentation cellars, packaging halls, keg-wash bays (PurCem 3/8" + slope to drain)
- **Dairies / cheese plants / yogurt** — wash-down + lactic acid + thermal shock = PurCem only
- **Pharmaceutical manufacturing + cleanrooms** — epoxy SL or MMA, ESD variants where required
- **Parking garages** — PU traffic-deck systems with crack-bridging membrane (Sika Sikalastic, Tremco Vulkem)
- **Mechanical rooms / penthouses** — mid-grade epoxy seal coat with broadcast aggregate for slip
- **Warehouses + light manufacturing** — epoxy SL or thin-mil seal coat with line striping
- **Hospital back-of-house** — soiled utility, sterile processing, pharmacy compounding, kitchens
- **Veterinary surgical + kennel** — seamless sanitary, integral cove, PurCem or epoxy SL
- **Locker rooms / shower rooms** — broadcast quartz with PU top, slope to drain
- **Auto-dealer service bays** — epoxy broadcast with PU top, oil-resistant, decorative
- **Cleanrooms / labs** — epoxy SL with ESD layer where required
- **Walk-in freezers** — MMA only (epoxy and PU won't cure cold)

## Best NOT used for

- **Hospital clinical patient zones** — sheet rubber or sheet vinyl wins on acoustics, underfoot comfort, and infection-control consultant preference; resinous is for back-of-house only
- **General office / retail front-of-house** — too utilitarian, too cold visually, too expensive vs LVT or carpet tile
- **Heritage spaces** — irreversible, hides historic substrate
- **Slabs with high MVER** (>3 lbs CaCl or >75% RH per F2170) without a moisture-mitigation primer — guaranteed delamination
- **Slabs with extensive moving cracks** — resin will telegraph and re-crack; address structurally first
- **Outdoor exposure without UV-stable aliphatic PU top-coat** — epoxy will yellow and chalk in months
- **Steam wash-down environments specced as plain epoxy** — wrong chemistry; thermal shock will pop the floor. PurCem is the answer.
- **Anywhere the slab is <28 days old** — concrete must be cured before any resinous goes down

## Substrate prep requirements

- **Slab age** — 28 days minimum cure on new concrete. Period. No exceptions.
- **Surface preparation** — shot-blast or scarify to **ICRI 310.2R CSP 3** (light epoxy seal), **CSP 4** (broadcast or SL), **CSP 5** (mortar or PurCem). Diamond-grinding is acceptable for SL but not for mortar.
- **Moisture testing** — ASTM F2170 RH probe **≤75% RH** (most systems) or ASTM F1869 CaCl **≤3 lbs/1000 sf/24 hr**. Some PurCem systems tolerate higher MVER — confirm against the specific TDS.
- **pH** — surface pH **≤9**.
- **Cracks** — **rigid epoxy crack injection** (Sika Sikadur 35, MasterInject 1500) for static cracks; do NOT bridge moving cracks with rigid epoxy.
- **Joints** — fill control joints with **semi-rigid polyurea** (Metzger McGuire MM-80, Sika Loadflex) or honour them through the resinous system per the manufacturer's joint detail.
- **Vapour drive** — when MVER exceeds the system limit, install an **epoxy moisture-mitigation primer**: **Aquafin VB-2000**, **Koster VAP-I**, **Ardex MC Rapid**, **Mapei Planiseal Easy**, **Sika Sikafloor EpoCem**. These typically tolerate up to 100% RH or 25 lbs MVER.
- **Containment** — **negative-air containment** with HEPA filtration in occupied buildings; **HEPA dust extraction on all shot-blast and grinding** equipment per **Ontario Reg 833 (Control of Exposure to Biological or Chemical Agents)** for respirable crystalline silica.
- **Climate control** — **10–30 °C** ambient and slab temperature, **RH ≤85%**, slab temperature minimum 5 °C above dew point at all times during cure (prevents amine blush on epoxy and condensation under PU).
- **Cure schedule** — typically 12–24 hr foot traffic, 48–72 hr light wheeled traffic, 5–7 days full chemical cure for epoxy and PU; **MMA: 1–2 hr foot, 2–4 hr full**; PurCem similar to epoxy.

## Maintenance

- **Daily:** dust mop or auto-scrub with neutral-pH cleaner; spot-clean spills immediately
- **Weekly:** auto-scrub with soft white pad and manufacturer-approved neutral cleaner; rinse
- **Periodic / restorative:** re-coat the urethane top every 3–7 years on high-traffic floors before wear breaks through to the body coat — this is the lifecycle-extender
- **AVOID — epoxy:** strong solvents (MEK, acetone), prolonged UV exposure, steam cleaning, sudden thermal shock
- **AVOID — PU:** strong acids and caustics depending on formulation; verify against chemical-compatibility chart
- **AVOID — PurCem:** very few restrictions — this is its whole point — but verify with manufacturer for unusual chemistries
- **AVOID — MMA:** strong solvents that re-dissolve the acrylic matrix
- **Inspection:** quarterly walk-through for delamination, edge lift, joint failure, slip-aggregate wear-through. Repair locally before damage spreads.
- **Lifecycle:** **epoxy 8–15 years** (longer with PU top-coat refreshes), **PU full system 10–18**, **PurCem 15–25** (the longest of the four — and the hardest to wear out), **MMA 10–20**
